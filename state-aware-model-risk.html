Project Report

Regime-Aware Model Risk Visualization

Diagnosing how predictive reliability and decision behavior shift across volatility-driven market regimes (SPY, daily).

Author: Hannah Attar
Asset: SPY (daily, 2006–2025)
Regimes: Gaussian Mixture Model (ret5, vol10, vol20)
Predictor: Logistic Regression (ret5, vol20)
Focus: Confidence reliability + regime-conditional deployment
Download PDF →

Abstract

Short-horizon equity return prediction is noisy, and models trained on pooled data can appear stable on average while behaving differently across market conditions. This project studies how predictive confidence and decision behavior vary across inferred market regimes and what that implies for deployment. Using daily SPY data, regimes are identified endogenously with a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) based on recent returns and realized volatility. A probabilistic logistic classifier is then evaluated out-of-sample by stratifying accuracy by confidence and regime. Finally, an illustrative strategy uses confidence filtering and regime-based deactivation to examine how selective deployment can alter drawdown and risk profiles, even when raw predictive performance is limited.

Highlights
  • Inferred market regimes via GMM using (ret5, vol10, vol20).
  • Evaluated a global probabilistic classifier with confidence defined as |p − 0.5|.
  • Documented regime-conditional accuracy patterns and feature-space separation.
  • Connected diagnostics to deployment via confidence filtering and regime deactivation.
  • Included calibration diagnostics (probability distribution, Brier score).

Results

The inferred regimes display clear temporal clustering, with the highest-volatility state concentrated around prolonged stress episodes. This persistence supports interpreting regimes as market conditions rather than transient noise.

Accuracy varies across regimes and does not increase monotonically with confidence. In the highest-volatility regime, high-confidence predictions exhibit substantial variability, indicating degraded reliability under turbulent conditions.

The equity curve illustrates how confidence filtering and regime-based deactivation primarily affect drawdown depth and return volatility rather than raw predictive accuracy. Results are gross of transaction costs.

Regimes occupy distinct regions of the ret5, vol20 feature space, implying that a single global decision boundary operates under materially different state distributions. This provides a geometric explanation for regime-dependent reliability.

Predicted probabilities are tightly concentrated near the unconditional mean, reflecting probability compression in a low signal-to-noise environment and motivating threshold-based deployment rather than probability ranking.

Key takeaways
  • Regime dependence: reliability varies across inferred market states.
  • Confidence filtering: thresholds are more defensible than probability ranking.
  • Risk control: regime-aware deployment mainly alters drawdown and volatility.
  • Calibration discipline: probabilities should be interpreted conservatively.

Calibration and Probability Compression

Predicted probabilities are concentrated near the unconditional mean, consistent with probability compression at the daily horizon. The out-of-sample Brier score of approximately 0.247 is close to an uninformative baseline, reinforcing conservative interpretation of probabilistic outputs.

Notes on Interpretation

This project is intentionally diagnostic rather than predictive. The primary contribution is identifying regime-conditional structure in confidence reliability and demonstrating how deployment rules can respond to that heterogeneity.
